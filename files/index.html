What's inside the housing

Three parts, one motion.

Petal-fold keypad

Five brass leaves sit flush against the door until you tap once — then they fold outward to reveal the code pad. No screen to smudge, no batteries to check monthly.

Solid brass cylinder

Machined from a single billet, not stamped sheet metal. It darkens with age the way a good doorknob should, and it shrugs off the salt air that kills most smart locks in two winters.

One-tap handoff

Send a guest key from the app in the time it takes to type a name. It expires itself — no follow-up reminder to revoke access after the dog-sitter leaves.

The unlock sequence

How a Tuliplock opens

  1. 01

    Approach

    The lock senses your phone or fob from about a metre out and wakes the cylinder — nothing visible happens yet.

  2. 02

    Tap the bloom

    Press the brass face once. The five petals fold open in under a second, the way a tulip opens over a warm morning, sped up.

  3. 03

    Turn and go

    The cylinder disengages, the door turns like any mechanical lock, and the petals close themselves behind you.

About Tuliplock

Built by two locksmiths and a bulb farmer's daughter.

Tuliplock started in 2019 in a shed behind a bulb farm outside Haarlem. Reinier and Femke Voss had spent a decade rekeying locks for a security firm and kept coming back to the same complaint: every "smart" lock they installed died within two winters of North Sea weather, or asked homeowners to trust a subscription they didn't want.

Their neighbor Ingrid, who ran the family tulip fields, showed them how a bulb's outer petals fold back to protect the bud through frost, then open on their own schedule once it's safe. That folding motion became the mechanism, not the metaphor — the petal-fold keypad is a direct copy of it, patented in 2021.

Every Tuliplock is still assembled by hand in a workshop that shares a wall with the original bulb shed. We test each cylinder through a real Dutch winter before it ships.
